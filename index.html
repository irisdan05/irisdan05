Lumeno AI: Accessibility Audit

A full accessibility audit and shipped feature set for an AI-powered study platform serving university students.

Role
Product Design Intern
Platform
iPad and Web
Timeline
Aug – Oct 2025
Team
CEO, 3 Engineers, 1 Designer (Me)
01) Overview

An AI tutor that was hard to learn from

Lumeno AI is an all-in-one study platform for university students, covering AI chat, flashcards, exam mode, summaries, and a digital notebook. The product was growing, but the core chat experience had a problem showing up consistently in beta feedback: the way AI responses were displayed made them genuinely difficult to read, particularly for students with ADHD or dyslexia.

Two weeks into my internship, I was tasked with running a full accessibility audit of the app and designing the features to address what came out of it.

02) My Role

Product Design Intern

I joined a team of around ten people across design, engineering, marketing, and product. The accessibility workstream was mine end to end: audit methodology, findings prioritisation, and the feature designs that shipped.

Accessibility audit WCAG evaluation Heuristic analysis Feature design Research synthesis
03) The Audit

Finding what the product was getting wrong

The audit ran across three methods: a WCAG 2.1 compliance review, a heuristic evaluation of the app's key flows, and synthesis of beta user feedback the team had already collected. That last source was particularly grounding. Students were already flagging the same problem repeatedly, so having real user signal alongside the guidelines review made prioritisation straightforward.

04) What We Shipped

Giving students control over how they read

The findings pointed to a consistent gap: students had no control over how information was presented to them. The design response was to build that control in, across the dimensions that mattered most.

Text size controls
Users can increase or decrease font size within the app, addressing the clearest compliance gap the audit surfaced.
Add screenshot: letter spacing control UI
Letter spacing controls
Adjustable kerning added alongside font size. For users with dyslexia, letter spacing can be the difference between readable and unreadable.

Audio and speech option
A text-to-speech feature was designed and shipped, giving students the option to have content read aloud. Particularly relevant for users with dyslexia and auditory learners.
05) Reflections

Accessibility is a product quality problem

The most useful reframe from this project was treating accessibility as a product quality issue rather than a compliance checklist. The WCAG violations mattered, but the stronger case for fixing them was that real students were struggling with the core feature of an app designed to help them study.

Designing around ADHD and dyslexia also sharpened how I think about information density more generally. The text clustering problem in Lumeno's chat affected everyone, just more acutely for users with attention difficulties.

SetDM AI

Designing a funnel-based inbox and AI co-pilot for fitness coaches and DM setters managing high-volume Instagram conversations.

Role
Founding Product Designer
Platform
Web app & Meta Integrations
Timeline
1 Month
Team
CEO, CTO, and 1 Designer (Me)
01) Overview

Instagram DMs were never built for scale

Instagram DMs rely on Instagram's inbox as their primary sales channel. The native inbox is flat, chronological, and completely unaware of where any given lead stands in a funnel. Coaches hire DM setters (people who make sales or set up sales calls using direct messaging on social media platforms) to manage the volume, but those setters had no real tooling to work with. The lack of pipeline visibility and assistance in reducing cognitive load make it painful to stage hundreds of conversations a day sustainably.

SetDM AI is a funnel-based workspace built with AI, made specifically for DM setters to make sales faster and without burning out.

02) My Role

Founding Product Designer

I spearheaded the product design, working in close partnership with the founder and engineer. The founder brought deep fitness industry and sales knowledge which shaped how we approached research and prioritisation throughout the design. The full initial product was completed within a month due to the high demand and limited time.

Information architecture Research AI Design Prototyping
03) Research & Discovery

Getting inside the DM sender's day

The research was done using structured interviews, a competitive analysis, and the founder's existing domain knowledge that came with DM setting experience too. Having the founder's industry background as a starting point meant interviews could focus immediately on edge cases and specific pain points rather than building basic context from scratch.

Competitive landscape

All four competitors shared the same blind spot: the setter was never the primary axis, and none combined pipeline structure with AI inside the inbox itself.

ManyChat
Automation-heavy, limited targeting tools. Not built for high-volume setter management or lead qualification.
Native Instagram
Flat inbox with no lead tagging, no funnel view, and no AI support. The baseline problem.
Revio
Focused on payments and subscriptions. No DM workflow or AI support for setters.
IntelliCoach
Coaching-oriented but built around session management, not DM-based sales pipelines.

04) Design Process

Designing a system, not just an inbox

The central design challenge was structural: getting coaches and setters a real-time read on their entire pipeline required every layer of the product to support it. Staging, funnel visibility, and message queuing all had to feed into one another rather than sit as separate features.

Since the one-month deadline meant phases overlapped heavily, sketching and shipping happened in the same sprint throughout. Reviews were shared regularly with potential users and the founder for feedback on practicality before anything went to engineering. This feedback process allowed us to ship fast and still get the results we wanted.

05) Key Decisions

Choices that defined the product

Funnel-first information architecture

In the early process, the question of who assigns conversation tags was a real design problem. Manual tagging by the coach created inconsistencies and friction. Having the AI handle categorisation automatically meant the setter's job becomes managing relationships rather than maintaining a system.

Adaptive AI replies

When autopilot is on, the AI works through a queued script, handling responses, following up on schedule, and progressing the lead through the funnel. When it is off, the coach is in full manual control. But to protect from potentially harmful responses, the AI surfaces flagged replies regardless of state. A coach can see exactly what the AI is planning to send and when, so the system never operates as a black box. That transparency was a deliberate trust decision as much as a design decision.

Keyword-based content flagging

Research made it clear that competitors had completely ignored setters being regularly exposed to hostile and inappropriate DMs with no way to protect themselves. We designed a dedicated flagging system to address this directly. AI scanning looks for potentially harmful content and routes anything suspicious to a separate Flags tab, notifying the setter without forcing them to read the full message. The setter can then access it with a layer of distance from content that was affecting their wellbeing, without losing the ability to review and act on it.

06) Outcomes

Early wins, promising scope for largest

SetDM AI is currently in invite-only early access. Onboarding is selective by design; the team works closely with those using it while the product is still being refined. Within those constraints, the results have been meaningful.

The setters in early access describe their day to the clearest signal. The job went from reactive and exhausting to largely autonomous. The product is still growing.

07) Reflections

Small wins, many unresolved insights

The finding that stayed with me most from this project was not about conversion rates or pipeline logic. It was the emotional nature of the job. People were managing hundreds of conversations a day through asking open questions and actually listening to the answers.

It reframed what the product was responsible for. The efficiency problems were real and solvable, but underneath them sat an empathy problem: the product was creating conditions where coaches and setters had to interact with hostile content. The flagging system felt different from how you'd normally think about moderation. It was a small surface area in the overall product, but it addresses a pain point that nobody else had bothered to design for.

Designing within a month, under real domain pressure, reinforced something about moving quickly: insight depends on who you are talking to. All the competitive analysis in the world would not have generated the friction map that came from sitting with real setters for an hour. It pushed me further toward tighter design sprint cycles, more open questions, and actually listening to the answers.
